Twenty-five years ago today marks the beginning of the darkest period in human history — a line drawn across all of time. A before, and an after.

It began without warning. Across the globe, inter-dimensional beings materialized from thin air. At first, we welcomed them — celebrating in the streets.

Then they kept coming. Millions of them — clogging transport routes, downing power grids, toppling cell towers. The world's governments united, and it was too little, too late. Nothing could stop the flood.

Within a year the global economy was in free fall — fuel shortages, mass unemployment, famine, civil unrest. Millennia of civilization brought to the brink. Not by war, not by plague, not by any disaster we'd ever feared.

But by something far more shocking…

THE HUFFLES.

THE KIDS WHO SAVED THE WORLD

recovered: handwritten, photographed, uploaded c.2010

The world's governments couldn't fix it. A bunch of kids did.

By 2010 the Huffle population was doubling and doubling again. Adults panicked — caged them, ran from them — and every frightened Huffle just made two more. The crisis fed itself.

The answer came from a leafy suburb outside Cincinnati — Madeira, Ohio — from kids who rode out to help instead of hide. They figured out what every expert missed:

> Happy Huffles don't multiply.

They wrote it down by hand — the Huffle Helpers Handbook — and put it online. Within a year it crossed the planet. The curve bent. The crisis broke.

A twelve-year-old named Cindy Sloane led that pack. The neighborhood watch she started has a name now, a building, and a budget in the billions.

It's called HRUMF.

Two kids started the Huffle Helpers on the same street in Madeira. Cindy Sloane was one. Her best friend — maybe more — was Chris Tanti. They drew the Helpers' first logo together.

Then a Huffle took everything from him. A terrified stowaway in the engine room of his parents' anniversary cruise. It ballooned. The ship went down — and his mother and father with it. Killed by the gentlest creature on Earth, because it was scared.

Cindy called it a tragedy. Chris called it a verdict. They were eighteen. They never spoke again.

Fourteen years later, Cindy runs HRUMF. And Chris runs the thing trying to destroy it.
