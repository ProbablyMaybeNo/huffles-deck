THE KIDS WHO SAVED THE WORLD

recovered: handwritten, photographed, uploaded c.2010

The world's governments couldn't fix it. A bunch of kids did.

By 2010 the Huffle population was doubling and doubling again. Adults panicked — caged them, ran from them — and every frightened Huffle just made two more. The crisis fed itself.

The answer came from a leafy suburb outside Cincinnati — Madeira, Ohio — from kids who rode out to help instead of hide. They figured out what every expert missed:

> Happy Huffles don't multiply.

They wrote it down by hand — the Huffle Helpers Handbook — and put it online. Within a year it crossed the planet. Communities started caring for their Huffles instead of fearing them. The curve bent. The crisis broke.

A twelve-year-old named Cindy Sloane led that pack. She's not twelve anymore. The neighborhood watch she started has a name now, and a building, and a budget in the billions.

It's called HRUMF.

Two kids started the Huffle Helpers on the same street in Madeira. Cindy Sloane was one of them. Her best friend — maybe more — was a boy named Chris Tanti. They drew the Helpers' first logo together.

Then a Huffle took everything from him. A stowaway, terrified, hidden in the engine room of his parents' anniversary cruise. It ballooned. The ship went down. His mother and father went with it — killed by the gentlest creature on Earth, simply because it was scared.

Cindy said it was a tragedy. Chris said it was a verdict. They were eighteen. They never spoke again.

Fourteen years later, Cindy runs HRUMF. And Chris runs the thing trying to destroy it.
